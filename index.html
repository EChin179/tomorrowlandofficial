the case for vertical architecture

when there's no where else to go, we go up.

Vertical Urban Planning:


  • Combats sprawl
  • Saves energy
  • Supports a growing population
  • Less traffic fatalities and jams
  • Preserves land for food production, nature, and recreation

...or down.

If we find that the sky may indeed have a limit, underground architecture fortunately allows us to go much further down than we can go up:


  • Reduce heat islands by minimizing the building footprint and installing green roofs
  • Reduce energy use (can save up to 5080%)
  • Reduce light pollution using low-reflectance surfaces
  • Control erosion, sedimentation, and runoff by covering the site with vegetation
  • Conserve existing natural areas, promoting biodiversity

the case for green architecture

from grey to green.

Green design approaches architecture as a means of minimizing any harmful effects of construction projects on human health and the environment. The mission of green architect is to safeguard the elements of air, water, and earth, by choosing eco-friendly and sustainable building materials and construction practices.

the ideal "green" building.

  • Ventilation systems with efficient heating and cooling
  • Landscaping with native vegetation with maximized passive solar energy
  • Minimal harm to the natural habitat
  • Powered through sustainable alternative energy sources
  • Uses non-synthetic, non-toxic, locally-obtained, responsibly-harvested material
  • Adaptive reuse of older buildings and recycled architectural salvage
  • Efficient use of space
  • Optimal location maximizing sunlight, winds, and natural sheltering
  • Rainwater harvesting and greywater reuse

the case for green transportation

modes of green trasnportation

Currently, most vehicles still utilize enormous amounts of energy that cause devastating amounts of pollution, negatively impacting the environment and our health. Fortunately, there are several methods of green transportation that enlist numerous benefits, including fewer to no environmental pollution, lower financial cost, the contribution to the building of a sustainable economy, and improved health.

Environmentally sustainable modes of transportation include:

  • Walking
  • Biking
  • Electric cars/bikes/motorcycles
  • Public transportation

implications on infrastructure.

While electric vehicles are a relatively excellent alternative fuel source compared to fossil-fuel powered cars, they unfortunately still indirectly cause pollution. Toxic elements within batteries can spew toxic fumes, and electricity generally isn't always generated sustainably.

Hence, the cleanest modes of transportation will always be walking, biking, and public transportation. To support an environment that encourages such habits, Tomorrowland must prevent urban sprawl, and have clean, wide, and accessible sidewalks, for both bikers and walkers. Public transportation will be critical to enforce accountability of citizens' habits, providing an accessible, clean, and efficient mode of transportation that should require no sacrifice on the part of the citizen in terms of comfort, security, and time.

the case for public transportation

importance of public transportation.

Encouraging citizens to move from high-emitting vehicles to more sustainable public transport will reduce the amount of emissions, improve air quality, and improve the health and standard of living for the population. As even electric vehicles leave some indirect carbon footprint on the Earth one way or another, encouraging a habit of utilizing public transport is vital to the sustainability of Tomorrowland.

maglev.

The term maglev is derived from 'magnetic levitation'. Maglev trains work on the principle of electromagnetic propulsion, wherein the cars are suspended, guided, and propelled using powerful magnets.


Pros

  • Wear and tear of parts is minimal, reducing maintenance cost
  • No rolling resistance, almost silent, and speed can be above 200 mph
  • Environmentally friendly (uses 30% less energy than normal trains and no internal combustion engines)
  • Weather proof
  • Safer than normal trains with state-of-the-art safety systems, keeping things in control even when at high velocity

Cons

  • High cost of development and construction
  • Requires unique set of tracks

micromobility.

Micro mobility is a category of modes of transport that are provided by very light vehicles such as electric scooters, electric skateboards, shared bicycles and electric pedal assisted, etc.


Pros

  • Allows people to easily commute short distances
  • Cheap and fun to ride
  • Better for the environment
  • Improves general health and wellbeing of people
  • Data of where vehicles are used and left can help build better cities
  • New jobs - collecting and charging e-scooters during the night

Cons

  • Somewhat unreliable - broken e-scooters
  • Easily wears by bad terrain, street conditions, and weather
  • May require frequent battery replacement

the hyperloop.

The hyperloop is a series of reduced-pressure tubes with pressurized capsules that can transport passengers at speeds of up to 700 mph. It is powered by an external electric motor, along with solar panels on the outside of the tube. Cheaper and more energy efficient than high speed rails, the hyper loop may be the best and cleanest solution so far to long distance travel.


Pros

  • Fast, efficient and cheap to operate
  • Average speeds of around 600 mph (970 km/h), with a top speed of 760 mph (1,200 km/h)
  • Low power consumption

Cons

  • High cost of development and construction
  • Susceptible to disruption from earthquakes, power outages, terror attacks
  • No sharp curves or abrupt height changes in the route
  • Vibration and jostling caused by high speeds
  • People will not be able to move freely during travel (no bathroom?)

electric, solar powered, autonomous buses.

E-buses are much better for the environment (depending on the cleanliness of their electric grid), with less vibration, less noise, and zero exhaust. They have lower operating costs and are overall easier to maintain. Solar panels can be installed on the top, which can help generate more electricity for the bus to run on. To minimize accidents, evolving technology can make the bus autonomous, creating less accidents on the road.

The only remaining issue would be that having an electric bus will require a complete do over of the bus system, requiring charging stations that are at least $50,000 a piece, not including the cost of new land.

the case for green infrastructure

elevated cycle paths.

This futuristic route would be made up of an enclosed, elevated tube, enabling cyclists to ride all year round in comfort thanks to full climate control. A network of elevated cycle paths towering above city streets would not only make cycling less dangerous and more convenient, but could reduce congestion on highways and lessen the amount of carbon emission greatly.

smart roads.

Over a million people a year are killed in road traffic accidents, with tens of millions more injured. Smart roads that are connected to the internet of things (IoT) can help reduce road deaths, as IoT-enabled road sensors can instantly communicate with smart cars about the best ways to avoid hazards or adverse road conditions. Additionally, wireless battery chargers can be installed underneath roads, reducing the level of air pollution and virtually eliminating the need for fossil fuels to power cars.

charging stations.

An electric vehicle charging station, or the EV charging station, supplies electric energy for the recharging of plug-in electric vehicles—including electric cars, neighborhood electric vehicles, etc. To support mass use of electric vehicles, charging stations should be easily accessible to everyone, ensuring the highest level of convenience for sustainable living.

the case for sustainable aviation

sustainable aviation fuels.

The global aviation industry produces around 2% of all human-induced CO2 emissions, and 12% of CO2 emissions from all transport sources, relative to 74% from road transport. Sustainable alternative fuels, or ‘biofuels’, are types of fuels that can greatly reduce the carbon intensity of air travel, produce fewer sulfur emissions, and can save up to 80% in emissions over the lifecycle of the fuel. They are derived from renewable sources, ranging from plant oils and solid municipal waste, to purpose-grown biomass and agricultural residues.

designing airports.

To be more eco-friendly, airports can be fitted with electric charging stations, rainwater capturing stations, and energy efficient LCD screens on monitors. They should be built from recycled building material, and have a heat reflecting roof, low-flow water faucets, and runways made of eco-friendly asphalt.

the case for orbital rings

orbital rings.

The orbital ring is a strong steel cable in orbit just above the atmosphere—80km above Earth. It rotates, creating forces which try to make the ring fly apart into space, while gravity tries to pull it down to Earth. If spun at the correct speed, the two forces balance one another, allowing it to rotate seemingly weightlessly. A “cuff” can be built around the cable which would hold itself in place, unmoving, by magnetic repulsion. The structure would be connected to the ground by cables, with an elevator giving access to the ring in less than an hour.

Two Maglev train tracks—using magnets to move trains without friction—on the underside of the ring and another on the outside could transport passengers at incredible speeds, reaching the other side of the world in 45 minutes.



...too ambitious?

Despite the seemingly outlandish nature of this solution, remember that the world’s current course towards the expansion of carbon-intensive air travel is just as unrealistic if we hope to avoid catastrophic climate change.

the case for renewable resources

the dark side to renewable energy.

Wind and solar are increasingly popular sources of energy, but the sun does not always shine, and the wind does not always blow. Renewable energy sources can be inconsistent, even at their best. Simultaneously, the pollution effects associated with creating solar panels, wind or hydroelectric turbines, and the materials needed to store large amounts of energy are tremendous.

Despite these shortcomings, there is a good chance that we will have to rely on renewable resources for a long while before dispensing of them completely. In the end, the goal will be less reliance on carbon-based fuel sources, and it is likely that a combination of solutions will make up our greener energy future.

more solar power?

Space-based solar power
Most solar energy doesn’t actually make it into the Earth’s atmosphere, so space-based solar power can help transcend that obstacle. The challenges would include the cost of getting a satellite to orbit, as well as the conversion of electricity into microwaves that can be beamed down to the planet’s surface.

Embeddable solar power
Solar window technology turns any window or sheet of glass into a photovoltaic solar cell that harvests the part of the light spectrum that eyes can’t see. If every window was made to collect some form of solar power, it could be a viable local source of energy for homes everywhere.

the case for alternative energy.

human power.

With over seven billion people walking the Earth today, harnessing energy and generating power from this movement could possibly be one of the energy sources of the future.

hydrogen power.

Hydrogen is a clean and potent source of energy, and accounts for 74% of the mass of the entire universe. The only problem is that hydrogen atoms tend to only be found in combinations with oxygen, carbon, and nitrogen atoms. Removing this bond takes energy, which ends up being counter-productive. As a result, many people around the world are working on making these processes more effective.

magma power.

The center of the Earth has the temperature of 6000 degrees Celsius, and tapping into that geothermal heat could very well be another possible source of energy for the future.

algae power.

Algae grows almost everywhere, they are also a surprising source of energy-rich oils. Up to 9,000 gallons of biofuel can be “grown” per acre, making it one of many potential energy sources of the future.

fusion power.

Fusion is the reaction that powers the Sun. It’s produced when two light atoms fuse into one under extreme pressure and temperature. Fusion has the potential to deliver much more power than nuclear fission, but without the long-lasting radioactive waste. With its potential of offering an inexhaustible supply of zero-carbon energy, nuclear fusion is still difficult to scale because the process still requires more power than it generates with existing technology.

Nuclear fusion as a power source has never been given the necessary funding to develop it to fruition, but it's the one physically possible solution to our energy needs with no obvious downsides. The physics works out beautifully; all that is needed is proper investment and engineering breakthroughs.

the case for waste management

trash talk.

Currently, waste management is a very linear process, and it is critical that it shifts to a more circular economy. This means that products need to be designed for disassembly at end-of-life. If usage of single-use plastics continues, then they should then be biodegradable in whatever environments they enter at end-of-life. The goods of today need to be seen as the raw materials of tomorrow. When that happens, products will begin to be made with the view of lasting longer and being easier to repair and dismantle.

There should also be an economical way to segregate waste. New technologies need to be developed to recycle specialized groups of waste, such as e-waste. Too many thrown away lithium ion batteries will eventually poison the environment.

food waste.

According to the Institution of Mechanical Engineers, half of the food produced around the world ends up thrown away. Smart measuring technology could charge consumers for the food waste they produce, which could help change the public’s attitude about food waste.

the case for waste to energy.

waste to energy plants.

Waste to Energy (WTE), is a term that is used to describe various technologies that convert non-recyclable waste into usable forms of energy including heat, fuels and electricity. It can occur through a number of processes such as incineration, gasification, pyrolysis, anaerobic digestion, and landfill gas recovery.

Recovering energy from the waste that remains after recycling is an important part of sustainable waste management and is key to reducing waste that would be sent to landfills.

metals recycling.

In the process of combustion, metals can be recovered from the remaining ash for recycling. This avoids a tremendous amount of greenhouse gases and recovers valuable natural resources that would have otherwise been lost in landfills. The production and mining of new steel, aluminum, copper, and other metals products is also a carbon intensive process that negatively impacts the environment.
